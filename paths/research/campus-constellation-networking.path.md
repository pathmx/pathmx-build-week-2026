# Campus Constellation

Networking is not collecting impressive people. It is learning how to begin, deepen, and continue useful relationships.

In this path, you will build a small “constellation” around one real college goal. Every practice creates something you can use: a question, an introduction, a conversation plan, a follow-up, or evidence that your confidence is changing.

> **Time:** 20 minutes to plan, followed by one small real-world mission.

## Networking Is a Learning Loop

UC Berkeley describes networking as a career conversation where people gather and share information. Harvard and Yale similarly emphasize informational conversations as a way to learn about roles, paths, and communities—not simply to ask for a job.

Use this loop:

1. **Wonder:** decide what you genuinely want to learn.
2. **Reach:** ask one person for a small conversation.
3. **Listen:** notice a story, insight, or next question.
4. **Return:** thank them, act on something useful, and reconnect when there is a reason.

Your first goal is not “build a huge network.” It is **have one useful, human conversation**.

## Make Your North Star Specific

Use the outcome you selected to complete one sentence:

> Over the next **___ weeks**, I want networking to help me **learn / decide / practice / find ___**.

Examples:

- “In four weeks, I want to learn what data analysts actually do during an ordinary workday.”
- “Before internship applications open, I want to decide whether product design fits my strengths.”
- “This semester, I want to become comfortable starting conversations with professors.”

Now define evidence that would make the goal real:

- conversations completed;
- questions answered;
- introductions or follow-ups attempted;
- confidence before and after each attempt;
- one decision or action changed by what you learned.

**Your North Star is allowed to change.** A conversation that changes your direction is progress, not failure.

## Set Your Practice Level

Choose the version that feels challenging but possible today. Your selection becomes the starting level for this practice.

- Warm-up — one low-pressure question
- Stretch — one five-minute conversation
- Expedition — one informational-conversation request

| Level | Best when | Today’s mission |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Warm-up** | Confidence is low or this is new | Ask a classmate one curiosity question after class |
| **Stretch** | You can introduce yourself but need practice continuing | Have a five-minute conversation with a professor, club member, or campus staff member |
| **Expedition** | You are ready for purposeful outreach | Request a 15–20 minute informational conversation with an alum or professional |

If your confidence is **1–2 out of 5**, start with Warm-up. At **3**, try Stretch. At **4–5**, choose any level based on your goal—not ego.

> **Coach encouragement:** “Choosing a smaller mission is not avoiding growth. It is designing a practice you can actually complete.”

## Map People by Perspective

Put your North Star in the center. Around it, name one possible person in each orbit:

| Orbit | Who might be here? | What could they help you understand? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Beside me** | Classmate, teammate, resident assistant | Shared experiences and opportunities you may have missed |
| **One step ahead** | Senior student, recent graduate, teaching assistant | Near-term choices and mistakes they remember clearly |
| **Guide** | Professor, advisor, coach, campus staff | Fields, resources, and ways to prepare |
| **Field signal** | Alum, practitioner, recruiter, community leader | Daily work, workplace context, and changing expectations |

Circle the person who is **most relevant and easiest to approach**, not the person with the most impressive title.

**Creative constraint:** your first constellation may contain only four people. Depth beats collecting names.

## Build a 15-Second Introduction

Create your introduction from three small pieces:

> **Present:** “I’m a second-year computer science student…”  
> **Curiosity:** “…exploring how network engineers keep systems reliable…”  
> **Bridge:** “…and your work on the campus network made me curious about what beginners often misunderstand.”

Put the pieces together, then adapt the length:

- **5 seconds:** name + shared context;
- **15 seconds:** present + curiosity + bridge;
- **30 seconds:** add one experience or reason the topic matters to you.

Avoid reciting your résumé. The introduction succeeds when it gives the other person an easy place to enter the conversation.

> **Coach encouragement:** “You do not need the perfect words. You need a clear reason for this conversation.”

## Ask Questions That Create a Conversation

Begin broad, listen, then climb toward detail:

1. **Story:** “How did you first become interested in this field?”
2. **Reality:** “What does a normal week look like that students rarely see?”
3. **Judgment:** “What separates someone who enjoys this work from someone who only likes the idea of it?”
4. **Action:** “What is one small project or experience you would recommend at my stage?”
5. **Bridge forward:** “Is there another perspective I should seek before making this decision?”

Choose only three questions. A good conversation follows what the person says instead of racing through a script.

**Listening challenge:** after each answer, ask one follow-up that begins with **what**, **how**, or **why**.

## The Awkward-Moment Simulator

Networking confidence grows when you know how to recover—not when nothing awkward happens.

Pick one card and respond aloud:

### They give a short answer

> “That makes sense. What part of that experience surprised you most?”

### You forget your next question

> “I want to think about that for a second. You mentioned ___—could you tell me more about it?”

### They are busy

> “No problem. Is there a better time, or would a short email be easier?”

### You do not know a term

> “I haven’t encountered that yet. How would you explain it to a student entering the field?”

### They cannot help

> “Thank you for letting me know. Is there a resource or type of person you would suggest instead?”

> **Coach encouragement:** “A graceful exit or honest question is a networking skill. Awkward does not mean unsuccessful.”

## Your Next 48-Hour Mission

Use the level you selected:

### Warm-up

Ask one classmate: “What helped you choose this course, club, or major?”

### Stretch

After class or at office hours, use your 15-second introduction and ask one Curiosity Ladder question.

### Expedition

Send a brief request:

> **Subject:** Student curious about your path into ___  
> Hello ___, I’m a ___ student exploring ___. I found your work through ___. Would you be open to a 15–20 minute conversation about your experience? I’m especially curious about ___. I understand if your schedule does not allow it. Thank you for considering it.

Set a deadline, location, and backup version. If the full mission becomes impossible, complete the next smaller version instead of abandoning the practice.

## Record What Changed

Immediately after the mission, capture five signals:

| Signal | Your evidence |
| --- | --- |
| Confidence before / after | ___ / 5 → ___ / 5 |
| One thing I learned | ___ |
| One moment I handled well | ___ |
| One skill to practice next | ___ |
| One action this conversation changed | ___ |

Do not score the interaction by whether it produced an internship, referral, or immediate reply. Score the behaviors you control: preparation, curiosity, listening, recovery, gratitude, and follow-through.

> **Coach encouragement:** “You created evidence by trying. Even a declined invitation teaches you that you can reach out respectfully and continue.”

## Turn Contact Into Continuity

Within 24 hours, send a short thank-you that proves you listened:

> Thank you for explaining ___. Your point about ___ changed how I’m thinking about ___. I’m going to try ___. I appreciate your time.

Then choose a **reason-based return**, not a calendar-only ping:

- you tried their advice and can share what happened;
- you found a resource connected to the conversation;
- you reached a milestone they helped shape;
- you have one thoughtful new question.

The relationship does not need constant maintenance. It needs honest context when you return.

## Let Evidence Choose Your Next Practice

- **If starting was hardest:** repeat a Warm-up with three different people and keep the same introduction.
- **If continuing was hardest:** practice the Curiosity Ladder and two follow-up questions.
- **If anxiety stayed high:** shorten the next mission and rehearse the awkward moment first.
- **If the conversation flowed:** move one orbit outward or ask for a longer informational conversation.
- **If your goal changed:** redraw the constellation around the new North Star.

Your weekly rhythm:

1. one conversation;
2. one useful follow-up;
3. one reflection;
4. one next mission chosen from evidence.

> **Coach encouragement:** “A network grows one remembered conversation at a time. You already know the next small move.”

The three saved choices—North Star, practice level, and hardest moment—give Codex enough evidence to update this path with a more personal next mission while keeping the curriculum readable outside the chat.

## Sources and Project Implications

### Source material

- [UC Berkeley Career Engagement: Networking](https://www.career.berkeley.edu/prepare-for-success/networking/) frames networking as career conversations for gathering and sharing information.
- [UC Berkeley Career Engagement: Informational Interviews](https://www.career.berkeley.edu/start-exploring/informational-interviews/) recommends preparing a brief introduction and open-ended questions while letting the conversation flow naturally.
- [Harvard College Guide to Making Connections](https://careerservices.fas.harvard.edu/resources/harvard-college-guide-to-making-connections-2/) describes introductions of different lengths and emphasizes tracking and maintaining connections.
- [Yale Office of Career Strategy: Networking and Informational Interviewing](https://ocs.yale.edu/stem-ppg-module-3-informational-interviewing-and-networking/) connects reflection, question preparation, peer feedback, and maintaining relationships.

### Observations

- Conventional networking advice often describes the correct behavior but gives learners little rehearsal for uncertainty, rejection, or awkward pauses.
- A college student’s nearest useful network already includes peers, teaching assistants, faculty, staff, supervisors, alumni, and community members.
- Counting contacts can reward shallow activity; evidence tied to learning, confidence, and follow-through better matches the learner’s goal.

### Implications for this project

- PathMX Blocks can make each networking move independently playable and reviewable.
- Stable block IDs can anchor future learner evidence without claiming that durable response persistence already exists.
- Codex can personalize the North Star, difficulty, scripts, simulations, and next route while PathMX preserves the resulting curriculum.

### Next experiment

Test this path with one college student. Observe whether they can select a realistic mission, complete it within 48 hours, and use their evidence to choose the next practice without needing a generic “network more” instruction.
